ADHO MUKHA SVANASANA

Downward-Facing Dog Pose

Adho Mukha Svanasana, commonly known as Downward-Facing Dog, is one of the most recognizable and essential poses in yoga. It functions as both a resting pose and a transition pose, effectively stretching the entire backside of the body while simultaneously strengthening the arms, shoulders, and legs. This mild inversion also helps to calm the nervous system.

SANSKRIT MEANING

The name literally translates from Sanskrit: Adho means 'downward', Mukha means 'face', and Svana means 'dog', translating to 'Downward-Facing Dog Pose'. The final shape of the body resembles a dog stretching.

ADHO MUKHA SVANASANA BENEFITS

  • Deeply stretches the shoulders, hamstrings, and calves.
  • Strengthens the arms, shoulders, and core muscles.
  • Calms the brain and helps to relieve stress and mild depression.
  • Energizes the body and improves circulation due to its mild inversion.
  • Helps to prevent and relieve symptoms of osteoporosis.
  • Improves digestion and provides therapeutic relief for headaches, insomnia, and fatigue.

CONTRAINDICATIONS

  • Carpal Tunnel Syndrome: The pressure on the wrists may aggravate this condition.
  • Late-stage Pregnancy: Avoid or use a modified version (hands on a wall or chair).
  • High Blood Pressure: Keep the head elevated or use a modified pose.
  • Chronic Shoulder or Neck Injuries: Consult a qualified yoga teacher for modifications to protect the joints.

ADHO MUKHA SVANASANA STEP-BY-STEP INSTRUCTIONS

Adho Mukha Svanasana is often entered from a tabletop position. Follow these steps for proper alignment:

  1. Begin on your hands and knees in a Tabletop position, with wrists directly under the shoulders and knees under the hips.
  2. Spread your fingers wide, pressing into the *base* of your index fingers and thumbs.
  3. Exhale, tuck your toes under, and lift your knees off the floor, raising your hips up and back towards the ceiling.
  4. Form your body into an inverted 'V' shape. Keep a slight bend in the knees if your hamstrings feel tight.
  5. Press your chest gently towards your thighs, drawing your shoulder blades down your back.
  6. Let your head hang naturally, making sure the neck is relaxed. Gaze towards your navel or between your feet.
  7. Keep your feet hip-width apart and gently press your heels toward the floor (they don't have to touch).
  8. Hold the pose for 5-10 breaths, focusing on steady, deep breaths.
  9. To exit, exhale and lower your knees back to the floor, resting in Child's Pose (Balasana).
